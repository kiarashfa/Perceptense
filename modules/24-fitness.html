Lifts, cardio, programming, and body mechanics

Part A · aerobic vs anaerobic — the fundamental split
Aerobic exercise
"With oxygen." At moderate intensities where oxygen supply meets demand. Fat and glycogen are the fuels. Can be sustained for long periods.
Fuel: Fat (primary at low intensity) + glycogen
Heart rate: 50–80% of max HR (roughly 220 − age)
Duration: Minutes to hours. Improves cardiovascular fitness, endurance, fat metabolism.
Examples: Walking, jogging, cycling, swimming, rowing, elliptical
Benefits: improved VO2 max, heart health, mitochondrial density, fat oxidation capacity, mood (endorphins, BDNF), longevity. The single best-studied intervention for long-term health is regular moderate aerobic exercise.
Anaerobic exercise
"Without oxygen." At high intensities where oxygen demand exceeds supply. Glucose is the primary fuel. Cannot be sustained long — produces lactate and fatigue.
Fuel: Glucose (glycolysis) + ATP-PCr system (very short bursts)
Heart rate: 80–100% of max HR. Can only be sustained seconds to ~2 minutes.
Duration: Seconds (sprint, max lift) to ~2 min. Builds strength, power, muscle mass.
Examples: Weightlifting, sprinting, HIIT, plyometrics, max-effort anything
Benefits: increased muscle mass, strength, bone density, metabolic rate, testosterone and growth hormone response, insulin sensitivity. After heavy resistance exercise, metabolic rate stays elevated for hours (EPOC — excess post-exercise oxygen consumption).
The lactate threshold: The intensity at which lactate production exceeds the body's ability to clear it — the transition from aerobic to predominantly anaerobic. Training this threshold (tempo runs, sustained efforts just below max) is the most powerful tool for improving athletic performance. World-class endurance athletes have extremely high lactate thresholds — they operate aerobically at intensities that would be anaerobic for most people.
The five heart rate training zones — what's happening physiologically
Zone 1
50–60% max HR
Active recovery. Fat is dominant fuel. Can hold a full conversation.
Zone 2
60–70% max HR
Aerobic base. Builds mitochondria and fat metabolism. "Endurance zone." Can hold a conversation.
Zone 3
70–80% max HR
Tempo/threshold. Glycogen is major fuel. Short sentences only. The zone where lactate begins accumulating.
Zone 4
80–90% max HR
Hard aerobic / above threshold. Can't speak comfortably. VO2 max improvement zone.
Zone 5
90–100% max HR
Max effort. Sprints, all-out intervals. Anaerobic. Seconds to a couple of minutes only.
The 80/20 rule: Elite endurance athletes spend roughly 80% of training time in Zones 1–2 and only 20% at Zone 4–5. Most recreational athletes do the opposite — they go moderately hard every session (Zone 3–4), which accumulates fatigue without developing either the aerobic base or the high-end capacity effectively. Zone 2 is boring but transformative over months. Zone 3 is the "grey zone" — hard enough to be fatiguing, not hard enough to maximise adaptation.
Part B · metabolism — how your body accounts for energy
Calculate your estimated daily energy needs
The calorie balance equation: Weight change = calories in − calories out. 1 kg of body fat ≈ 7,700 kcal stored energy. A 500 kcal/day deficit = ~0.5 kg/week loss. A 500 kcal/day surplus = ~0.5 kg/week gain. This is the physics — but metabolism adapts (BMR drops with sustained deficit, rises with sustained surplus), so the relationship is not perfectly linear over time.

Part D · breathing — when to inhale, when to exhale, why it matters
The universal rule for resistance training
Inhale on the eccentric phase
The eccentric is the lowering/lengthening/easier phase — where you're resisting gravity. Breathe in here. Examples: lowering the bar to chest in bench press; descending into a squat; lowering yourself on a pull-up; descending in a deadlift.
Exhale on the concentric phase
The concentric is the lifting/shortening/harder phase — where you're doing the work against gravity. Breathe out here. Examples: pressing the bar up in bench; standing up from a squat; pulling yourself up on a pull-up; lifting the bar in a deadlift.
Why this matters — intra-abdominal pressure (IAP): Exhaling during the hardest part of a lift (concentric) reduces intra-abdominal pressure, which reduces spinal stability — making the lift weaker and increasing injury risk. The technical technique for heavy lifting is the Valsalva manoeuvre: take a deep breath, brace your core hard (like you're about to be punched), hold the breath through the sticking point of the lift (the hardest moment), then exhale as you complete the rep. This creates maximum IAP and spinal stability.
Squat breathing cue
Inhale and brace before descent. Hold breath through the bottom and begin exhale as you drive up. Complete exhale at top. Reset breath each rep for heavy sets.
Deadlift breathing cue
Stand tall, take a deep breath, brace 360° around your torso. Initiate the pull. Begin exhale as bar passes knees. Exhale fully at lockout. Lower the bar, reset.
Light/cardio breathing
For lighter weights and cardio: breathe naturally in rhythm with the movement. Don't hold breath. For running: try 2-count or 3-count breathing (inhale for 2–3 strides, exhale for 2–3 strides).

The science of muscle growth — what actually drives it
The signal: mechanical tension
Mechanical tension (load on the muscle, especially under stretch) is the primary driver of hypertrophy. When a loaded muscle is stretched under tension (the eccentric phase), mechanosensors in the muscle fibre detect this and trigger the anabolic signalling cascade (mTOR pathway).
The process: muscle protein synthesis
Exercise triggers muscle protein synthesis (MPS) — the construction of new protein to repair and add to muscle fibres. MPS is elevated for 24–48h after resistance exercise. Without adequate dietary protein, the body can't build the new tissue regardless of training stimulus.
The limit: genetics and testosterone
Natural muscle gain: ~0.5–1 kg/month for beginners, ~0.25–0.5 kg/month intermediate, ~0.1–0.25 kg/month advanced. Men gain faster than women (testosterone). Genetic ceiling exists — "natural" bodybuilders hit a ceiling set by their genetics and hormone profile.
Protein timing — does it matter?
The "anabolic window" (eat protein immediately post-workout) is largely a myth for people eating adequate daily protein. Total daily intake matters far more than timing. That said, distributing protein over 3–4 meals maximises MPS — each meal should contain ~20–40g protein to optimally stimulate MPS.
Part F · recovery — the most undervalued variable

Why muscles grow on rest days, not training days

Training is the stimulus, not the adaptation

Exercise creates microscopic damage in muscle fibres (microtears — especially from eccentric loading). The repair process — which happens primarily during sleep and rest — makes the fibre thicker and stronger than before. Training without recovery produces more damage than repair. "Overtraining" is genuinely possible — it suppresses immune function, raises cortisol chronically, and can reverse progress.

Minimum recovery time by muscle group

48–72 hours between sessions for the same muscle

Large muscles (legs, back): 72h minimum between heavy sessions. Smaller muscles (arms, shoulders): 48h. High frequency (hitting a muscle 3x/week) works only if volume per session is lower. Signs of insufficient recovery: persistent soreness, performance declining session to session, disrupted sleep, mood changes, loss of motivation.

Sleep's role in muscle growth

~70–80% of growth hormone secreted in N3 sleep

N3 (deep) sleep triggers the largest GH pulse of the day. GH drives tissue repair and protein synthesis. Consistently sleeping less than 7 hours: impaired recovery, lower testosterone (~10–15% reduction after one week of 5-hour nights), elevated cortisol, and reduced muscle protein synthesis. Sleep is arguably as important as training for body composition.

Progressive overload — the only principle that matters long-term

Gradually increasing the demand on the muscle

The body adapts to any given load and stops responding. Progress requires progressive overload: increasing weight, reps, sets, range of motion, or reducing rest time over time. Without it, training maintains but doesn't improve. The simplest implementation: add a small amount of weight (2.5–5 kg) when you can complete all reps of all sets with good form.

DOMS — delayed onset muscle soreness

Not an indicator of a good workout

DOMS peaks 24–72h after exercise, especially after eccentric-heavy movements or novel exercises. It is inflammation from microscopic damage — not a measure of training quality. You can have an excellent, muscle-building session with little DOMS (particularly as you become more trained). Chasing soreness is not a training strategy. DOMS is not related to lactic acid — that clears within ~30 min of exercise ending.

Overtraining vs underrecovery

Most "overtraining" is actually under-eating and under-sleeping

True overtraining syndrome (systemic physiological breakdown from excessive training volume) is rare in recreational athletes. More common: functional overreaching — a temporary state of fatigue where performance drops, usually from training hard while eating in a deficit and sleeping poorly. Fix: eat more, sleep more, reduce training volume temporarily. Signs of real concern: persistent performance decline, resting heart rate chronically elevated, mood disturbance, illness frequency.

Part H · rep ranges, sets & the logic of programming
Rep ranges and what they train — the fundamental programming table
Reps % of 1RM Primary adaptation Rest between sets Practical note
1–3 93–100% Max strength (neural) 3–5 min Trains the nervous system to recruit more motor units. Very low fatigue per rep but high CNS demand. Best for strength athletes. Injury risk is highest — requires perfect technique.
4–6 85–93% Strength + hypertrophy 2–4 min The strength-hypertrophy overlap. Used in powerlifting accessory work and by athletes wanting both adaptation types. High mechanical tension stimulus.
6–12 67–85% Hypertrophy (primary) 60–120 sec The classic bodybuilding range. Optimal balance of mechanical tension, metabolic stress, and volume. Most hypertrophy research is conducted in this range.
12–20 60–67% Hypertrophy + muscular endurance 45–90 sec Effective for hypertrophy when sets are taken close to failure. Higher metabolic stress. Good for isolation exercises and joints that prefer lighter loads (knees, elbows).
20+ <60% Muscular endurance 30–60 sec Minimal hypertrophy unless taken to absolute failure. Develops capillary density and lactate tolerance. Useful for conditioning work, high-rep bodyweight movements.
The range is less important than proximity to failure. Research by James Krieger and others shows hypertrophy occurs across all rep ranges (6–30) when sets are taken close to failure (1–3 reps in reserve). A set of 20 reps with 2 reps left in the tank is roughly as effective for muscle growth as a set of 8 reps with 2 reps in reserve — as long as the load is sufficient. What this means practically: don't worry too much about hitting exact rep targets. Stop when form breaks down or 1–2 reps remain, whatever the number.
One-rep max (1RM) estimator — and what each percentage means for training
Formula: Brzycki (1RM = weight × 36 / (37 − reps)). Most accurate for 1–10 reps. Estimates become less reliable above 10 reps. Never attempt a true 1RM without a spotter and substantial training experience.
Training programme structures — which split is right for you?
How you distribute training across the week matters less than total weekly volume per muscle group. The best split is the one you'll actually do consistently.
Part I · cardio programming — distance, intervals, and the VO2 max

VO2 max — what it actually measures

mL of O₂ per kg per minute

VO2 max is the maximum rate at which your body can consume oxygen during exercise. It is the single best predictor of cardiovascular health and aerobic fitness. Elite male distance runners: 70–85 mL/kg/min. Elite female: 60–75. Average sedentary adult male (25–35): ~35–40. Average female: ~30–35. Recreational runner: ~45–55. It declines ~1% per year after age 30 without training — but regular Zone 2 training can maintain or even improve it well into middle age.

HIIT — high-intensity interval training

Short bursts, outsized adaptation

HIIT alternates periods of maximal or near-maximal effort with recovery. A classic protocol: 4×4 (4 minutes at ~90–95% max HR, 3 minutes active recovery, repeat 4 times). A Tabata set is 8 rounds of 20 seconds on / 10 seconds off (4 minutes total). HIIT produces significant VO2 max improvement in less time than steady-state cardio — but is harder to recover from. 2× per week is a common upper limit. More is rarely better.

Running pacing — a practical sense of speed

Easy pace: you can talk in full sentences

Easy run: 60–65% max HR — feels genuinely easy, full conversation. Moderate: 65–75% — short sentences. Tempo run: 75–85% — a few words only, comfortably hard. Race pace varies: recreational 5K ~25–30 min, 10K ~50–60 min, half marathon ~2–2:30h, marathon ~4–5h. Elite marathon: under 2:10 (men) / under 2:20 (women). A rough rule: your easy run pace should be 60–90 seconds per km slower than your 5K race pace.

Cardio vs weights for fat loss

Calories out: cardio wins per session. Long-term: combination wins.

Running 5km burns ~300–400 kcal. A 45-minute strength session burns ~200–300 kcal during, but EPOC adds 100–300 kcal over 24–48h. More importantly: muscle tissue raises resting metabolic rate (~13 kcal/day per kg of muscle). 10 kg of added muscle = ~130 kcal/day more without exercise. Over a year this compounds significantly. The evidence is clear: resistance training preserves lean mass in a calorie deficit, cardio alone tends to lose both fat and muscle.

Part K · common myths & what the evidence actually says
❌
"Lifting makes women bulky"
Women have ~10–20× less testosterone than men and significantly less growth hormone. The hormonal environment for dramatic muscle growth simply doesn't exist. Female competitive bodybuilders achieve their physique after years of specific, high-volume training often combined with performance-enhancing drugs. Resistance training for women produces strength, bone density, improved body composition, and metabolic benefit — not bulk.
❌
"You can spot-reduce fat"
Doing 500 sit-ups will not burn the fat on your stomach. Fat loss is systemic — the body decides where to draw down fat stores based on genetics and hormones, not which muscles you work. The stomach, hips, and thighs tend to store fat preferentially (especially in women) and are the last places to lean out. Core exercises build core muscles; they do not "burn" overlying fat. Only a calorie deficit reduces fat, and where it comes off first is not controllable.
❌
"Muscle turns into fat when you stop training"
Muscle and fat are different tissues — one cannot convert into the other. When you stop training: muscle undergoes atrophy (the fibres shrink) due to lack of mechanical stimulus. Simultaneously, if you continue eating the same amount without the energy expenditure of training, you gain fat. These two things happen at the same time, giving the appearance of one "turning into" the other. They are independent processes.
❌
"Cardio kills your gains"
The "interference effect" (cardio blunting muscle growth) exists but is heavily overstated by the fitness internet. Meta-analyses show that concurrent training (cardio + weights in the same program) reduces hypertrophy by only ~5–10% compared to resistance training alone — and only when cardio volume is excessive (long sessions, same day as lifting, same muscle groups). 3–4 moderate cardio sessions per week alongside resistance training does not meaningfully impair muscle growth and improves health markers significantly.
❌
"You need to feel the burn / soreness to have trained well"
The burn (metabolic acidosis from lactate accumulation) and soreness (DOMS from eccentric damage) are both side effects of training, not measures of effectiveness. An experienced lifter can have a highly productive session with minimal soreness. Chasing the burn leads to prioritising metabolic stress over mechanical tension, which is a less effective stimulus for strength and hypertrophy. Quality of movement and progressive overload matter far more than how much something hurts.
❌
"Static stretching before exercise prevents injury"
Static stretching (hold a stretch for 30–60 seconds) before exercise has been shown to temporarily reduce muscle force output, reduce peak power, and reduce sprint performance — the opposite of what you want before lifting or sprinting. The evidence for injury prevention from pre-exercise static stretching is weak. The effective warm-up approach: dynamic movements (arm circles, leg swings, bodyweight squats, light sets of the exercise you're about to perform). Static stretching is valuable — just save it for after training, or as a standalone flexibility session.
Part L · test yourself

1. You weigh 80 kg and want to build muscle. You're currently eating 100g of protein per day. Is this enough, and what foods get you to the right amount?

No — 100g is insufficient for muscle building at 80 kg. The research consensus for muscle hypertrophy is 1.6–2.2g per kg of body weight per day. For 80 kg: 128–176g protein/day. The minimum meaningful target is 128g; 160g is a solid working target. The gap between 100g and 160g = 60g of additional protein daily. Practical sources per 100g of food: chicken breast (~31g), canned tuna (~25g), Greek yoghurt (~10g per 100g, or ~17g per 170g pot), eggs (~13g per 2 eggs), cottage cheese (~11g per 100g), lentils (~9g per 100g cooked), whey protein shake (~25g per scoop). A rough daily structure: 2 eggs at breakfast (13g), 150g chicken at lunch (46g), 170g Greek yoghurt as snack (17g), 150g salmon at dinner (45g) = 121g. Add a protein shake (25g) = 146g. This is achievable with deliberate food choices without supplements, though protein powder makes it much easier.

2. What is the difference between a squat and a deadlift — why do both exist, and which muscles does each primarily work?

They target overlapping but distinct movement patterns. The squat is primarily a knee-dominant pattern — the knee flexes and extends under load, with the torso relatively upright. Primary movers: quadriceps (front of thigh), glutes, with hamstrings and adductors supporting. The bar (or weight) is above the centre of mass, requiring upper back and core to stabilise. The deadlift is primarily a hip-dominant pattern — the hip hinges (bends), with the spine in a neutral position and the bar on the floor. Primary movers: hamstrings, glutes, erector spinae (lower back), with quadriceps helping to break the floor initially. Secondary: traps, lats (to keep bar close), forearms (grip). Why both exist: the squat develops anterior chain (quads) and vertical pushing power; the deadlift develops posterior chain (hamstrings, glutes, back) and pulling strength. Together they cover essentially all major lower body and hip muscles. A programme with both is more complete than either alone.

3. What is EPOC and why does it make post-workout calorie calculations complicated?

EPOC (Excess Post-exercise Oxygen Consumption) is the elevated oxygen consumption — and therefore elevated calorie burn — that persists after exercise ends. After aerobic exercise, EPOC lasts 15–60 minutes and adds a modest number of calories (50–150 kcal). After high-intensity resistance training or HIIT, EPOC can last 24–48 hours and add 100–350 kcal. The complication for calorie tracking: fitness trackers and apps calculate calories burned during exercise based on the movement, but they don't account for EPOC. More importantly, heavy resistance exercise has a smaller "during exercise" calorie burn than cardio (because rest periods are included) but significantly larger post-exercise metabolic elevation. This is why pure calorie-during-exercise comparisons favour cardio but miss the long-term metabolic advantage of resistance training. The practical implication: two workouts with the same calorie burn during exercise may have very different total metabolic costs once EPOC is included.

4. Why does the number on the scale sometimes go up right after starting a new exercise program, even if you're eating correctly?

Several things happen simultaneously that can increase scale weight despite positive body composition changes. First, glycogen storage: exercise depletes muscle glycogen, and as the body adapts to regular training it stores more glycogen. Each gram of glycogen is stored with ~3g of water. Increased glycogen storage can add 1–3 kg of water weight within the first 2–4 weeks. Second, muscle inflammation: resistance training causes microscopic damage and inflammation that draws fluid into the muscle tissue (the "pump" partially, and post-exercise swelling). This inflammation peaks around 24–48h post-exercise. Third, muscle protein synthesis genuinely adds mass. This is slow but real. The scale captures all of these — fat, muscle, water, glycogen, food in the digestive tract. For this reason, bodyweight trending over 4+ weeks is more useful than daily measurement, and body measurements or progress photos are more informative than scale weight alone for tracking actual body composition change.

5. You've been lifting for 6 months and your progress has stalled — same weights, same reps. What is almost certainly wrong, and what should you change?

A plateau after 6 months almost always has one of three causes, often in combination. First and most common: absence of progressive overload. If you've been lifting the same weight for the same reps for months, the muscle has fully adapted to that stimulus and has no reason to grow further. Fix: systematically add small amounts of weight (2.5 kg) or reps each week, or add a set. Second: insufficient protein. Muscle growth requires building blocks. If you're not consuming 1.6–2.2g protein/kg/day, you can stimulate muscle all you want but can't build it. Fix: track protein for two weeks and hit the target. Third: insufficient recovery. If you're training hard but sleeping 6 hours, stressed, or undereating, the recovery gap limits adaptation. Fix: prioritise sleep, ensure calorie intake is at or above maintenance if building is the goal. A less common fourth: your programme needs structural change (adding volume, changing exercises to create novel stimulus, adjusting rep ranges). True overtraining causing a plateau is rare — the other three causes are far more common.

6. What does "training to failure" mean, and should you do it?

Training to failure means performing reps until you physically cannot complete another rep with proper form (technical failure). "Reps in reserve" (RIR) is the alternative language — 1 RIR means stopping with 1 rep left in the tank. Research shows that going to failure and stopping 1–3 reps short of failure produce very similar hypertrophy — the last few reps of a hard set are the most stimulating, but absolute failure isn't required to get that stimulus. Practical considerations against always training to failure: (1) it significantly increases recovery time, (2) it increases injury risk (form breaks down at failure), (3) it makes it harder to maintain technique across multiple sets, (4) CNS fatigue accumulates. The modern evidence-based approach: most working sets should end 1–2 reps short of failure, with occasional sets taken to failure on low-risk exercises (machines, cables, isolation movements). Never train to failure on technical free-weight movements like squats and deadlifts.

7. Is there any meaningful difference between free weights and machines for building muscle?

For pure hypertrophy (muscle size), machines and free weights produce similar results when sets are taken close to failure at matched volumes. The practical differences are significant however. Free weights (barbells, dumbbells) require stabiliser muscles to control the movement, train neuromuscular coordination, and are more transferable to athletic performance and real-world strength. They also have a steeper learning curve and higher injury risk when technique is poor. Machines provide guided movement (less stabiliser demand), allow isolation of specific muscles, are safer for training close to failure (no fear of dropping a bar), and are easier for beginners to learn. The practical optimal approach: compound free weight movements (squat, deadlift, bench, row) as the foundation, with machines and cables as accessories for isolation and volume. The internet debate about "free weights vs machines" is largely irrelevant — what matters is progressive overload applied consistently over time.
